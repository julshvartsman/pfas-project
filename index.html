The Map

Research Question

Who bears the burden of PFAS contamination in America, and who decided they would?

Project Framing

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS), better known as "forever chemicals," are an extensive family of synthetic fluorocarbons linked to immunity deficiency, cancer, birth defects, and hepatic and kidney disease. These chemicals accumulate in the environment, making exposure a long-term risk.

Using an environmental justice framework, this project investigates PFAS exposure in drinking water and asks whether that exposure is linked to race, class, age structure, and rurality. The goal is to determine not only who is burdened by PFAS contamination, but also which systems of power allowed that burden to fall where it did.

Environmental Justice Framework

The project draws on critical race theory and intersectional feminism to examine how environmental harms are distributed and hidden along racial and socioeconomic lines. Robert Bullard's environmental justice framework centers the right of all people to live, work, play, go to school, and worship in a clean and healthy environment.

This approach also asks how databases and visualizations carry the interests, goals, and biases of the institutions that produce them. In that sense, the map is not only a technical object; it is a way to make structural inequality visible.

Component 02
The Record

Data & Analysis

A guided record of how contamination data is retrieved, joined, filtered, and interpreted. This section pairs visual analysis with the limitations and ethical concerns that shape what the data can and cannot claim.

Methods

The pipeline retrieves PFAS measurements from the EPA's PFAS Analytic Tools, which surface UCMR-5 drinking water testing data for public water systems. It filters to readings at or above each analyte's Minimum Reporting Level, the threshold below which laboratories cannot reliably confirm the concentration detected.

Because UCMR reports ZIP codes served by water systems while ACS demographic data is published for ZIP Code Tabulation Areas, the project applies a ZIP-to-ZCTA crosswalk. Each contamination record is joined to ZCTA geography and ACS 5-year estimates, then aggregated to a mean PFAS concentration per ZCTA and reporting period.

Data Limitations

Differences across ZCTAs reflect not only contamination, but also where testing happened. UCMR monitoring thresholds favor large public water systems and often exclude private wells. Coverage is uneven, so under-monitored areas, often rural and lower-income, can appear artificially clean.

Because this missingness may correlate with the demographics being studied, the project treats "not tested" as a distinct category rather than a low or absent value. The ZIP-served field is also an approximation, because a system may serve only part of a listed ZIP code.

Ethical Considerations

Data visualization compresses people and stories into aggregates, sometimes obscuring the human context behind the numbers. The story highlights are included to surface some of what aggregation can hide, while recognizing that many affected communities still remain outside the project's narrative frame.

The team also recognizes its positionality as outsiders working with public datasets and institutional methodologies. The project does not control the underlying data collection, only the visualizations and analysis built on top of it.

Human & Community Impact

"These stories, along with our data visualizations, illuminate the human decisions and power dynamics behind the environmental crisis of PFAS contamination."
Project narrative analysis

Narrative Analysis

A 2023 Harvard study by Jahred Liddie and collaborators found that predominantly Black and Latino communities saw higher PFAS concentrations in drinking water. The study connected this pattern to nearby industrial buildings, military plants, and airports clustered near communities with less political and economic power to challenge those decisions.

The following case studies trace patterns that may not be fully captured through data analysis. Together with the map and record, they foreground the decisions, institutions, and power dynamics behind contamination.

Visualizing Burden

Newburgh, New York

In 2016, New York discovered PFOS contamination in Washington Lake near Newburgh and traced the source to Stewart Air National Guard Base. Residents near the area reported PFOS blood concentrations five times the average after likely consuming contaminated water for decades.

Newburgh is a majority Latino and Black city facing economic marginalization. Similar to the Harvard study discussed above, this case shows how PFAS exposure can follow existing lines of racial and economic inequality.

The pattern identified by Liddie and collaborators appears to repeat nationally: marginalized communities are impacted by decisions made around them and for them, often before they are given the information or resources needed to protect themselves.

This connects to the project's environmental justice framework because Newburgh residents lacked a meaningful opportunity to prevent PFAS exposure before the damage was done. The case helps turn the map's aggregate patterns into a human story about uneven power, delayed disclosure, and unequal protection.

Source: Shantal Riley, "The New York Water Crisis That Nobody's Talking About," VICE, 21 Jan. 2020.

Visualizing Cost

3M, DuPont, and Withheld Knowledge

In 1997, Kris Hansen was hired by 3M to investigate PFOS. Her tests on blood bank samples showed that PFOS was already widespread, while internal records revealed that 3M had known about harmful consequences and had hidden reports and studies.

This story reveals the intention and causation behind the PFAS crisis. Contamination from PFAS was not simply accidental; it was shaped by companies and organizations prioritizing efficiency, liability management, and cost over public health.

Those with power, including those with early knowledge of contamination, were able to externalize risk onto communities with far less ability to fight back. In the context of this project, the question of who decides these consequences points to a structure that allows harm to continue at the expense of people with less institutional power.

The pattern extends beyond 3M. A 2023 study of documents from DuPont and 3M found that both companies' laboratories identified PFAS toxicity decades before that information was publicly declared. Through a data feminism lens, this is a clear example of how data can carry the interests and goals of its creators. Company research was produced, withheld, and structured in ways that protected the companies rather than the public.

Sources: Nadia Gaber et al., "The Devil They Knew," Annals of Global Health, 2023; Sharon Lerner, ProPublica, 2024.

Visualizing Negligence

Marinette, Wisconsin

Residents near a fire training facility in Marinette were exposed to PFAS contamination for years before being notified by Arcadis, a consulting firm hired to investigate water sources. When residents sought answers, authorities advised against blood tests, citing the lack of effective treatments.

Residents were left with the results of years of contamination, unexplained illnesses, and a system that knew there were problems but did not solve them. This reveals another layer of PFAS harm: the damage is not only physical, but institutional.

Stories like Marinette, where a gap exists between institutional knowledge and public disclosure, are exactly what this project aims to make visible. Under an intersectional feminist framework focused on environmental justice, data visualizations need to be paired with narratives about institutional neglect.

That pairing helps the audience see how people with power and resources can remain indifferent to hidden struggles in affected communities.

Source: Thomas W. Pearson, "Communities Grapple With Exposure to 'Forever Chemicals,'" SAPIENS, 31 July 2020.

Visualizing Accountability

Hoosick Falls, New York

After his father died of kidney cancer, Michael Hickey noticed illness patterns in Hoosick Falls and tested the water himself after local authorities declined to do so. The testing revealed PFAS contamination tied to a local manufacturing plant.

Settlements were eventually issued to residents, but the lives lost were permanent consequences of neglect. The fact that a resident could test the water himself shows that one of the central problems around PFAS contamination is accountability.

Institutions had the responsibility and capacity to act, yet community members were forced to fill the gap. This is just one example of how an unaccountable system, which decides residents' fate for them, can leave lasting impacts on the communities it is meant to serve.

From a data feminism perspective, Hickey's work also illuminates the labor behind exposing the PFAS crisis. The responsibility to collect, test, and present data ethically fell to the community that public institutions were supposed to protect. This case asks who has to do the work of making harm visible when official systems fail.

Source: William Brangham, "How a Small Community Fought for Justice After Finding Forever Chemicals in Drinking Water," PBS NewsHour, 13 Nov. 2025.

Glossary
PFAS
An extensive family of synthetic fluorocarbons linked to immunity deficiency, cancer, birth defects, and hepatic and kidney disease.
PFOS
A PFAS historically used in firefighting foams and stain repellents, linked to immune, developmental, and thyroid harms.
UCMR
An EPA program requiring public water system testing every five years to monitor emerging drinking water pollutants.
ZCTA
A Census Bureau statistical geography used to approximate ZIP code areas for demographic analysis.
Minimum Reporting Level
The lowest concentration a laboratory can reliably measure and report using an analytical method.
American Community Survey
A Census Bureau survey program collecting socioeconomic, housing, and demographic data.
CDC Social Vulnerability Index
A metric evaluating community resilience to external stressors using Census variables.
Environmental Justice
The principle that all people have the right to safe, healthy environments and fair environmental governance.
Environmental Racism
The disproportionate exposure of marginalized racial groups and communities to environmental hazards.
Critical Race Theory
An academic framework examining how race and racism are embedded in institutions and laws.
Intersectional Feminism
A framework examining how overlapping identities shape experiences of oppression and discrimination.
Bioaccumulation
The gradual buildup of toxic substances within an organism over time.
Public Water System
A utility or organization that provides drinking water to the public.
Missingness
The condition of values being absent from a dataset.
Geo-polygon
A closed geographic region represented by latitude and longitude coordinates.
Positionality
The social and political context that shapes a person's identity, perspective, and worldview.
ZIP-to-ZCTA Crosswalk
A tool that maps USPS ZIP codes to Census ZIP Code Tabulation Areas.
Data Feminism
An approach to data science that applies intersectional feminist theory to data collection, analysis, and interpretation.
